Unit I

Poisson Distribution — Arrival Process

The Poisson distribution models the number of arrivals in a fixed time period. In queueing theory, customers arrive at a rate λ (lambda) per unit time. The probability of k arrivals is P(X=k) = e-λλk/k!. The inter-arrival times follow an Exponential distribution with mean 1/λ.

Unit II

Conditional Distribution — Waiting Time Estimation

Conditional distributions allow us to estimate waiting times given known system states. In an M/M/1 queue, the conditional distribution of wait time W given that k customers are already present determines service quality. P(W>t | N=k) = e-μ(k+1)t for exponential service. Bayes' theorem links prior queue state to posterior wait time.

Unit III

Markov Chain — State Transitions

A Markov chain models the queue state at each discrete step, where the future state depends only on the current state. In M/M/1/K queues, states represent the number of customers {0,1,2,...,K}. The birth-death process with birth rate λ and death rate μ defines the transition matrix.

Unit IV

Cross-Correlation — Arrival vs Departure

The cross-correlation function RXY(τ) = E[X(t)·Y(t+τ)] measures the statistical relationship between the arrival process X(t) and departure process Y(t) at lag τ. In a stable queue, departures are correlated with arrivals at positive lags (departure follows arrival). Peak correlation occurs at the mean service time.

Correlation Interpretation

τ < 0: No causal link. Departures don't precede arrivals.
τ = 0: Simultaneous correlation due to shared service pool.
τ = 1/μ: Peak — departure most likely exactly one service time after arrival.
τ → ∞: Correlation decays to λ² (independent processes).
Unit V

Linear Systems with Random Inputs

A linear system with impulse response h(t) processes the random arrival signal X(t) to produce output Y(t) = h(t) * X(t). The output PSD is SY(f) = |H(f)|² · SX(f), and the output autocorrelation RY(τ) = h(τ) ★ RX(τ). The queue acts as a low-pass filter smoothing the Poisson input.
